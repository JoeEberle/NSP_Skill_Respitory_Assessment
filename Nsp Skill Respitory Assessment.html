Steps for Respiratory Assessment:

  1. Preparation:
  2. Ensure the scene is safe and use standard precautions.

  3. Introduce yourself and obtain permission to assist.

  4. Observe Breathing:

  5. Look and feel for the chest to rise and fall.

  6. Assess Key Aspects:

  7. Rate: Count the breaths per minute.
  8. Rhythm: Check if the breathing is regular or irregular.
  9. Depth: Note if the breaths are shallow or deep.
  10. Effort: Observe for any signs of labored or effortless breathing.
  11. Noise: Listen for unusual sounds, such as wheezing, gurgling, or stridor.

Characteristics of Normal or Good Breathing (Adults and Children):

  1. Rate:
  2. Adults: 12 to 20 breaths per minute

  3. Children:

    • Infants (0-1 year): 30 to 60 breaths per minute
    • Toddlers (1-3 years): 24 to 40 breaths per minute
    • Preschoolers (3-6 years): 22 to 34 breaths per minute
    • School-aged children (6-12 years): 18 to 30 breaths per minute
    • Adolescents (12-18 years): 12 to 20 breaths per minute

  4. Rhythm:

  5. Regular and even: Breathing should have a consistent pattern, with no sudden pauses or irregularities.

  6. Smooth and continuous: Pauses between breaths should not be prolonged or erratic.

  7. Depth:

  8. Moderate and consistent: Each breath should involve a steady rise and fall of the chest or abdomen, depending on the age group.

  9. Age Considerations: Infants and young children often breathe with their abdomen, which is normal.

  10. Effort:

  11. Effortless: Breathing should be easy, without visible strain.

  12. No use of accessory muscles: In adults, children, and infants, there should be no exaggerated use of neck, shoulder, or abdominal muscles. Straining, nasal flaring, or grunting can indicate difficulty breathing.

  13. Noise:

  14. Quiet and gentle: Breathing should be silent or very faint.
  15. No abnormal sounds: There should be no wheezing, stridor, gurgling, or other sounds that indicate a problem with airflow.
